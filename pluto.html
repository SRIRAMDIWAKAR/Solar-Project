Pluto

Pluto, considered to be the ninth planet by many, is actually more of a dwarf planet. It was discovered by Clyde Tombaugh, who identified it in 1930 as part of our solar system's family of planets until the International Astronomical Union redefined the criteria for what constitutes a planet in 2006. ### Key Facts about Pluto: - **Diameter**: Pluto is estimated to be about two-thirds the size of Earth's moon, with a diameter of approximately 2,377 kilometers (1,477 miles). - **Orbit**: It has an elliptical orbit and completes one orbit around the Sun in about 248 Earth years. - **Surface**: The surface is made up mainly of nitrogen ice, and some regions on Pluto have methane or carbon monoxide ice. There are mountains, plains, and possible cryovolcanoes. - **Atmosphere**: Pluto's atmosphere is very thin and mostly composed of nitrogen. There are traces of methane and carbon monoxide as well. Its atmosphere also changes in size with its orbit around the Sun. - **Moons**: Pluto has five known moons. In fact, the largest moon is called Charon, and when it was discovered relative to Pluto's size, its discoverers opted to consider them a double dwarf planet system. ### Exploration It was during the fly-by by the New Horizons spacecraft of NASA in July 2015 that high-resolution close-up images and data about this dwarf planet were brought back, which portray its complex geology and its life-giving atmosphere. Pluto is an object of strong interest to planetary scientists and continues to be a topic of discussion about classification and status in our solar system.
